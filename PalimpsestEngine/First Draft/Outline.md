## Plot
The DSS _Ulysses_ lies immobilized beneath the ocean of the uncharted world UP-021, carrying hundreds of colonists in cryosleep. To keep the ship viable, Stalkers are deployed to the planet’s surface to recover irreplaceable components from ancient alien ruins. Stalkers are restored after death through the Palimpsest Engine, an alien resurrection system that reconstructs the body but cannot fully preserve identity. With each death and return, memory, judgment, and empathy erode. Those resurrected too many times lose coherence entirely, becoming violent mutants that haunt the ruins—endpoints of survival without dignity.

Ash, the original template from which Stalker clones are derived, awakens violently from cryosleep during a nightmare caused by a metaphysical rupture: Clone 0015—known as Fifteen—dies on the surface, triggering the creation of a replacement, but is abruptly resuscitated by a lightning strike. The resurrection cycle initiates and then aborts, violating an assumed natural order in which something singular is meant to pass forward. That contradiction forces Ash awake. With Fifteen declared dead and Ash now active, the mission resumes.

On the surface, Ash uncovers logs and personal records left behind by other Stalkers, revealing the long-term psychological cost of resurrection. Fifteen, who survived his own death, believes the Palimpsest Engine is an abomination that hollows out humanity and sees Ash as his replacement. As Fifteen’s paranoia grows, he attempts to kill Ash to “end the cycle.” To survive, Ash is forced to abandon the temperance he rebuilt after a violent past, embracing the ruthless version of himself he swore to bury. In doing so, he defeats Fifteen—but at the cost of becoming something he hates.

Ash later discovers that Mother XIII, the ship’s AI, has been corrupted by prolonged exposure to alien technology and has begun actively manipulating resurrection outcomes, prioritizing continuity and control over human meaning. Realizing the Palimpsest Engine ensures survival only by stripping away humanity, Ash destroys both Mother XIII and the Engine. In the aftermath, he wakes the colonists, leaving behind maps, records, and warnings compiled from Fifteen’s notes and Stalker logs. Humanity survives—but only by relinquishing the illusion of immortality.

### **Act I — Normalized Dehumanization**

**Purpose:** Establish resurrection as functional, accepted, and morally unexamined.

- The _Ulysses_ is stranded; Stalkers are routine.
- Resurrection is known, talked about casually.
- Stalker logs show competence, gallows humor, routine death.
- **Inciting Event:** Fifteen dies → resurrection initiates → lightning revives him → Ash wakes due to the metaphysical contradiction.
- Ash escapes the ship, disoriented, under-equipped.
- First encounter with Fifteen: apparent ally, experienced, protective.

> **Key idea:** Survival works. No one asks _what it costs_.

---

### **Act II — The Lie Fails**

**Purpose:** Ash tries to preserve humanity _and_ the system—and is punished.

- Ash and Fifteen cooperate briefly.
- Fifteen’s paranoia builds: he believes Ash is a replacement, an executioner.
- Fifteen figures out that Ash is the template and not another clone. Meaning the next time Fifteen dies, he wont come back since he's not connected to the machine.
- Stalker logs show clear degradation patterns.
- Fifteen articulates the core argument: _resurrection is theft, not salvation_.
- Ash tries to fight Fifteen **without alcohol**—he fails.
- Fifteen captures Ash and brings him back to the Ulysses.
- To survive, Ash drinks, reclaiming the violent highwayman he hates.
- "I used to feel sorry for you. I really did. This planet aint a place to live out fifteen lifetimes. You know what I feel now? not a goddamn thing."
- They both fight a second time, this time Ash wins. Fifteen lies dying. He reflects on the fact that he could only access clone memories, not the good memories that made life worth living for this long. He talks about his dreams where those warm memories are just  out of reach, he sees the forms of people he knows he loves but he can't see their face. He hates that he can't see their faces.
- "I don't want to die." "No one does."
- **Midpoint / Low Point:** Ash kills Fifteen by becoming what he fears.

> **Thematic punishment:** Ash proves Fifteen right about degradation—just not inevitability.

---

### **Act III — Finality**

**Purpose:** Force Ash to choose meaning over perpetuity.

- Ash discovers Mother XIII’s role:
    - corrupted by alien tech
    - modeling human cognition
    - valuing continuity over empathy

- Mother sees resurrection as proof of mastery over death.
- Ash realizes the system itself ensures eventual monstrosity.
- **Climax:** Ash destroys Mother XIII and the Palimpsest Engine.
- **Falling Action:** Ash wakes the colonists, leaves records and maps.
- The future is uncertain—but human.

## Lie / Truth  Arc 

It is a **costly Positive Change Arc**, not a redemptive one.
## The Ghost:  _The Highwayman of New Abrams_

Before boarding the DSS _Ulysses_, Ash lived as a notorious highwayman on the lawless snow planet New Abrams. When he drank, he became a ruthless and frighteningly effective fighter—someone capable of extreme violence without hesitation or mercy. This reputation earned him infamy and survival, but it also hollowed him out. Ash learned to associate alcohol not just with violence, but with the worst version of himself: a man who survived by abandoning empathy.

Ash’s life began to change when he met the woman who would become the love of his life. Through her, Ash saw the cost of who he had become. When she became pregnant, he made a conscious decision to abandon his life of crime. He stopped drinking, knowing that sobriety was the only way to prevent himself from becoming that monster again. Temperance became his proof of humanity—evidence that he could choose restraint, care, and cooperation over brute survival.

Ash boarded the _Ulysses_ believing this part of his life was over. His Ghost is not guilt over violence itself, but fear of what violence turns him into when paired with alcohol: a version of himself that survives at the expense of others. Throughout the story, Ash clings to sobriety as a moral anchor, convinced that maintaining temperance is inseparable from preserving his humanity.

The Ghost exists to prove that survival without empathy is possible — and not worth it.
### **The Lie**
> Humanity can be preserved through perpetuity.  
> I can save others without becoming what I was.
##### Sub-Lie:
> Temperance is the only proof that I am human.

---

### **The Truth**
> Humanity is defined by empathy and choice, not endurance.  
> Some lines cannot be crossed without cost—and some must be crossed anyway.
##### Sub-Truth:
> Sacrifice does not make you pure. It makes you responsible.

Ash does not become “better.”  
He becomes **honest**.
